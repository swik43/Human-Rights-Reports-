# Country: South Sudan

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

South Sudan seceded from Sudan in 2011 after nearly two decades of civil war. A political crisis followed, leading to a conflict that erupted in December 2013 and led to more than 1.3 million internal displacements in 2014. A peace agreement was signed in 2015 but the conflict escalated again in 2016 and by the following year the country had the highest number of IDPs on record. The government and several non-state armed groups signed a revitalised peace agreement in 2018, but implementation has been slow and insecurity has persisted.


Repeated violations of the agreement were reported in 2022 as violence continued to escalate, triggering nearly 337,000 displacements, most of them in Jonglei, Upper Nile and Unity states. Almost 1.5 million people were living in displacement as a result of conflict of violence at the end of 2022, the highest since the peace agreement was signed.


Some analyses have suggested a correlation between climate anomalies, localised violence and increased displacement risk in South Sudan. In addition to heightened climate variability, cattle raids and attacks against civilians by armed cattle keepers further disturbed pastoralists’ habitual mobility patterns and triggered at least 257,000 displacements. These dynamics, which often have an ethnic dimension, represent a challenge to the implementation of the revitalised peace agreement.


The country was also hit by severe floods for the fourth year in a row, which hampered the provision of humanitarian aid and increased food insecurity. The floods triggered nearly 596,000 displacements, an increase from the 2021 figure which was already the highest disaster figure in more than a decade.


Rains and floods were prevalent throughout the year and by October, two-thirds of the country were under water. The floods affected displaced communities living in camps and some built dikes to prevent further displacement. Impassable roads impeded the provision of food and other aid to more than 460,000 IDPs in Bentiu, the capital of Unity state. Humanitarian organisations had to adopt other means of delivery, including by air and boat. Between July and October, however, non-state armed groups increased the number of checkpoints along White Nile river. They looted convoys and extorted organisations for money and supplies.


The consecutive years of widespread flooding also mean that livelihood opportunities such as planting during the lean season are no longer an option for people in affected areas. People displaced by floods were also at risk of waterborne diseases, and most who fled conflict were living in circumstances far below the minimum emergency thresholds for survival. IDPs sheltering in the bush and swamps faced the worst conditions. Many of those affected by the floods were unable to return home by the end of 2022. In total, around 665,000 people were still living in displacement as a result of disasters, 26 per cent more than 2021. Together with people displaced by conflict and violence, the overall figure reaches more than 2.1 million people, a 13 per cent increase from 2021.
