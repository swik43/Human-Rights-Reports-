# Country: Ukraine

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Conflict and violence have triggered most internal displacements in Ukraine in recent years, particularly since 2014 when Russia annexed the autonomous region of Crimea and non-state armed groups seized parts of the eastern regions of Donetsk and Luhansk, declaring independence. This prompted a military response from the Government of Ukraine, and the ensuing fighting triggered a high number of internal displacements, particularly in 2014, 2015 and 2016.


Despite several ceasefire agreements, hostilities in eastern Ukraine have continued since, leading to several further waves of displacement. The latest ceasefire was agreed in July 2020, but the situation deteriorated again in 2021. The number of security incidents recorded each month reached pre-ceasefire levels by the middle of the year and continued to increase until the end of it.


The shelling of a frontline settlement in November was the only event identified as having forced people to flee their homes in 2021. It triggered 40 displacements, the lowest number since 2014. That said, there were still around 854,000 people thought to be living in protracted displacement as result of the conflict at the end of the year.


Russia began to amass troops near its border with Ukraine in the second half of 2021, and by the end of the year it had mobilised 100,000 soldiers and significant military hardware. It launched a full-scale invasion in February 2022, plunging the countryinto the world’s largest and fastest evolving internal displacement crisis (read more ).


Disasters also forced people from their homes in 2021. Record-breaking rains in Crimea and the regions of Zaporizhzhia and Odesa in June and July led to flooding that  triggered  2,000 displacements.


The government of Ukraine adopted a law on internal displacement in 2014 and established the Ministry of Reintegration of the Temporarily Occupied Territories in 2016. It has since allocated funds to address displacement, expanded IDPs’ access to social services, facilitated their registration, pension payments and movement across the contact line and amended legislation to compensate those whose homes have been destroyed. The Cabinet of Ministers approved a new strategy on IDPs’ integration.
