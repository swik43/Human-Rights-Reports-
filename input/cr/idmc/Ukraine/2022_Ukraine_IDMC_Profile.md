# Country: Ukraine

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Conflict and violence have triggered most internal displacements in Ukraine in recent years, particularly since 2014 when Russia annexed the autonomous region of Crimea and non-state armed groups seized parts of the eastern regions of Donetsk and Luhansk. This prompted a military response from the Government of Ukraine, and the ensuing fighting triggered 1.7 million internal displacements between 2014 and 2021.


The conflict took on new dimensions when Russia invaded Ukraine on 24 February 2022. Shelling and airstrikes hit major towns and cities, including the capital, Kyiv. The situation escalated quickly, triggering nearly 16.9 million movements in 2022. Many were repeated displacements of people unable to earn an income or find accommodation, or those seeking safety and access to services. The figure represents 60 per cent of all internal displacements associated with conflict and violence recorded globally in 2022.


Displacement and return patterns shifted with the ebb and flow of the violence. Some people sought protection from airstrikes and shelling, while others moved away from the rapidly shifting frontlines across the north, east and south of the country. At the end of July 2022, the government called for the mandatory evacuation of civilians in the Donetsk region to prevent further casualties.


Evacuation calls were soon extended to other frontline areas such as Kharkiv, Kherson, Mykolaiv and Zaporizhzhia. They were scaled up at the end of the summer in part in preparation for winter because people were at risk of being left with no heating or basic services to survive the cold. Authorities had organised the evacuation of more than 350,000 civilians from dangerous areas by the end of November. Many others, unaccounted for, risked evacuation by their own means.


As of December, 74 per cent of IDPs thought that return to their places of origin would offer the best prospect of a long-term solution to their displacement. Such intentions were unfeasible for many, however, as a result of insecurity, the presence of explosive remnants of war, absence of services and livelihood opportunities or the belief that their homes had been destroyed or were under the control of Russian forces.


Around 5.9 million people were thought to be living in internal displacement across Ukraine as of 5 December 2022. Many others crossed the border, bringing the number of refugees to eight million. The rapidly changing situation has made data collection difficult, and information from occupied areas including Crimea and parts of the east is hard to come by, meaning that internal displacement figures should be considered conservative.


Housing became a major issue in host areas in the months after the invasion as it became clear the conflict would become prolonged. In cities such as Lviv, where more than 200,000 people sought shelter in the first two weeks of the conflict, housing prices soared, putting pressure on local communities and markets. The cost of food, medicine and fuel also increased across the country as a whole.


The government of Ukraine adopted a law on internal displacement in 2014 and established the Ministry of Reintegration of the Temporarily Occupied Territories in 2016. It has since allocated funds to address displacement, expanded IDPs’ access to social services, facilitated their registration, pension payments and movement across the contact line and amended legislation to compensate those whose homes have been destroyed. The Cabinet of Ministers approved a new strategy on IDPs’ integration and medium-term solutions to displacement in October 2021. The strategy, which runs until 2024, is intended to provide access to administrative and social services, employment and housing. In 2022, one of the Ministry of Social Policy’s first actions after the invasion was to improve IDPs’ ability to register in their new places of residence.


IDPs and their hosts also played a remarkable role in responding to the crisis. Networks of volunteers proliferated, and community-based initiatives were scaled up across the country. These were useful in delivering more timely aid to those in need, particularly in areas most affected by conflict and inaccessible to humanitarian organisations. Free of larger organisations’ bureaucratic procedures, they expedited the supply of medicines, food and other items.
