# Country: Syria

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Around 6.7 million people in Syria were living in internal displacement as a result of conflict and violence at the end of 2021, the highest figure worldwide. Around 80 per cent of displaced households have been uprooted for four or more years. Disasters including floods, storms and drought also trigger displacement and force many people already displaced by conflict to flee again.


Last year marked a decade since the beginning of the Syrian war. Conflict and violence triggered 456,000 internal displacements, the lowest since 2012 and a significant drop from the 1.8 million recorded in 2020. Fighting ebbed across the country as a whole, but hostilities intensified in frontlines in the northwest, northeast and south, driving displacement. The south-western governorate of Dara’a experienced several waves of violence as clashes between the Syrian armed forces and non-state armed groups reignited. The violence led to more than 38,000 displacements before a deal between the warring parties in September brought about a de-escalation. At the height of the hostilities, power was cut and other public services were hampered. Humanitarian access was limited. A significant majority of those displaced were women and children who were left living in precarious conditions. By September, 28,000 people were able to return, but many returned to areas with explosive hazard contamination and extensive damage to housing and public infrastructure.


Hostilities further intensified in the north-western governorate of Idlib, which has become one of the most densely populated areas of the country as IDPs have arrived en masse over the past decade. Renewed airstrikes and shelling in southern areas of the governorate triggered nearly 12,000 displacements in June 2021 in the largest escalation of violence and displacement since a ceasefire was signed in March 2020. Insecurity and a deterioration of the socio-economic conditions across the country also forced people to flee their homes.


Disasters triggered 79,000 displacements country-wide, most of them the result of floods, gales and snowstorms. Thousands of tents in displacement camps were destroyed, forcing IDPs to move again. The north-east of the country suffered its worst drought in more than 70 years, and water scarcity has increased food insecurity, deepened the humanitarian crisis and aggravated socioeconomic difficulties, particularly among IDPs, many of whom have resorted to negative coping mechanisms. Internal displacement due to drought was identified but there were no reliable estimates available.


The country is in the throes of a protracted displacement crisis. Ongoing fighting, insecurity, violations of international humanitarian law, and disasters continue to drive displacement. Pervasive instability, lack of livelihoods, a worsening drought, and a rise in fuel and food prices are aggravating the socio-economic crisis across the country. Insecurity in IDP camps, informal settlements and collective centres is further deteriorating. IDPs’ ongoing humanitarian needs are acute and their prospects of durable solutions still elusive, which calls for renewed efforts in addressing the world’s largest internal displacement crisis.
