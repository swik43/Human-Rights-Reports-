# Country: Libya

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

When the conflict in Libya started in 2011, more than 498,000 people were internally displaced. After three years of violence and further displacement, a contested election in May 2014 resulted in two separate governments and warring factions. The civil war that ensued triggered 950,000 displacements in the following years, as many people were forced to flee their homes.


Hostilities subsided in late 2020 after the UN mediated a ceasefire agreement between the parties to the conflict. Returnees have outnumbered the number of people displaced ever since as improved security has facilitated progress towards durable solutions. Around 670,000 IDPs have been able to go back to their areas of origin, leaving 160,000 still displaced as of the end of 2021, the lowest figure since 2014.


Last year was the first in a decade in which no new or secondary displacements due to conflict were recorded. It was a year of hope for the country as the peace process and planning for presidential and parliamentary elections in December progressed, and humanitarian needs decreased significantly.


The elections, however, were postponed and the country’s remaining IDPs still have only limited access to essential services such as healthcare, safe drinking water and adequate housing. Returnees have the greatest needs, mainly for food and water, and many have gone back to homes that are severely damaged. National funds to help IDPs and returnees rebuild their communities are still to be approved, and it is unclear what compensation or support they will receive.


Disasters triggered 1,200 displacements in 2021. Flash floods between October and December caused severe damage to homes in Tripoli and Marj, and disrupted the provision of IDPs’ basic services including clean water, food, shelter and health.
