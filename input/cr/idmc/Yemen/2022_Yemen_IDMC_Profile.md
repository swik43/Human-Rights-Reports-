# Country: Yemen (North Yemen)

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

The humanitarian crisis in Yemen remains one of the most acute in the world. More than half the population is assessed to be acutely food insecure and humanitarian access is heavily restricted. Many IDPs continued to suffer from repeated displacement, often due to a combination of conflict and disasters. Some have attempted to return, but their prospects are limited by the constant threat of renewed violence and a lack of livelihood opportunities.


The ongoing conflict affecting the country can be traced back to long-standing political, economic, and social tensions, particularly between the north and the south. In 2011, during the Arab Spring, widespread political unrest, instability and violence triggered about 175,000 internal displacements. Conflict between Ansar Allah, also known as the Houthi movement, and the government grew in intensity, and, in late 2014, the movement captured Yemen’s capital, Sana’a. In response, a Saudi-led coalition of Arab states launched a military campaign in 2015 to reinstate the ousted government.


Clashes between Ansar Allah and the coalition continued to trigger a high number of displacements, in 2022, especially in the first quarter, with almost 71,000 movements recorded in January alone. Attacks against humanitarian staff and administrative impediments hindered access to and delivery of life-saving assistance to 21.6 million Yemenis in need. On 2 April, a two-month truce came into effect as a result of UN-facilitated negotiations. It was extended twice until 2 October, contributing to a more than five-fold reduction in internal displacements from an average of 48,000 from January to March, to an average of 9,100 from October to December 2022. The truce was not renewed, but large-scale fighting had not reignited as of the end of 2022. Still, nearly 276,000 displacements were recorded for 2022.


Durable solutions are also still out of reach for many. When comparing the six months preceding to the six months following the truce, returns to Hodeidah increased four-fold, while those to Marib decreased by half, but lack of data made it unclear how safe and sustainable they were. Persistently high levels of insecurity and the lack of livelihood opportunities in return areas also discourage many IDPs from returning, preferring instead to wait for clear signs of peacebuilding before they go back to their homes. Local integration could be a potential solution for many IDPs, but it has been hindered by a significant increase in forced evictions, partly because IDPs are unable to afford rent in their host areas.


Disasters also triggered a high number of movements, totalling over 171,000, which represent 82 per cent of flood displacements in the MENA region. The majority were the result of flash floods during the rainy season. Hodeidah, Marib and Hajjah governorates were the most affected, particularly during the June-to-August rainy season. The rains were 300 per cent above the average annual intensity and swept across 80 per cent of the country, forcing many people already displaced by conflict to flee again. As during the 2020 floods, water sources were contaminated, reducing access to safe drinking water and increasing the risk of waterborne diseases. The situation was particularly severe for IDPs living in camps, which tend to lack adequate water and waste management infrastructure.


Although the truce brought a reduction in fighting, Yemen continues to record persistently high levels of new and repeated movements, and an increasing number of people living in displacement. The figure for the end of 2022 stood at over 4.5 million IDPs, one of the highest in the world and second only to Syria in the MENA region.
