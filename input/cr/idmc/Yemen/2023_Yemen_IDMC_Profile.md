# Country: Yemen (North Yemen)

---

## Year: 2023

### Country Profile

The conflict affecting Yemen can be traced back to long-standing political, economic, and social tensions, particularly between the north and the south. In 2011, during the Arab Spring, widespread political unrest, instability and violence triggered about 175,000 internal displacements. Conflict between Ansar Allah, also known as the Houthi movement, and the government grew in intensity, and, in late 2014, the movement captured Yemen’s capital, Sana’a. In response, a Saudi-led coalition of Arab states launched a military campaign in 2015 to reinstate the ousted government. The conflict has continued since, triggering hundreds of thousands of displacements every year.


On 2 April 2022, a UN-facilitated two-month truce between the Saudi-led coalition and Ansar Allah came into effect. Although it expired six months later, elements of it were generally observed in 2023, with no major escalations reported. The number of internal displacements by conflict and violence was its lowest in a decade as a result, at 80,000. Sixty per cent of them were reported in the frontline governorates of Hodeidah and Taiz.


Yemen was also among the countries that reported the highest displacement figures associated with disasters in the Middle-East and North Africa, with 240,000 movements. Flood-related displacements totaled 174,000 and were mainly concentrated in the coastal governorates of Hajjah, Taiz and Hodeidah. The same governorates have reported significant levels of conflict and disaster displacement in recent years.


Tropical cyclone Tej also struck the country on 23 October. The storm hit the eastern governorates of Al Maharah and Hadramawt, triggering 65,000 movements and making it the largest storm displacement event in Yemen since records began in 2008. Tej caused floods that destroyed homes and infrastructure, hampering many people to return.


IDPs in Yemen continued to face significant humanitarian needs, including for adequate shelter and food. Economic challenges undermined their capacity to overcome the vulnerabilities associated with their displacement, which explains why the number of people living in displacement as a result of conflict or disasters as of the end of 2023 remained at 4.5 million.
