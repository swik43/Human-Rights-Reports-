# Country: Afghanistan

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Afghanistan faces one of the world’s most acute internal displacement crises. The country has suffered conflict and insecurity for more than four decades and experiences recurring disasters and climate shocks. Displacement is a common coping strategy for many Afghans and, in some cases, an inevitable feature of life across generations.


Conflict between the Taliban and government forces escalated in 2021, culminating in the Taliban taking control of Kabul. The fighting triggered more than 723,000 new displacements, the highest figure ever recorded for the country. The majority took place between May and August as tens of thousands of people fled provincial capitals including Kandahar, Kunduz, and Lashkar Gah.


Many of the newly displaced began to return to their places of origin from September as the conflict in the provinces died down. After 40 years of war, however, damage to homes and infrastructure is widespread, returnees’ and IDPs’ needs are high, and the deteriorating economic and humanitarian situation continues to prompt displacement both within and cross-border.


Disasters triggered more than 25,000 displacements in 2021. The most significant incident occurred in May, when flooding prompted 16,000 in 14 provinces. The following month the government officially declared drought, with 80 per cent of the country suffering severe or serious dry conditions and 20 per cent moderate conditions. These aggravated food insecurity and water scarcity nationwide and are likely to have forced many people from their homes, but reliable data was hard to come by. Severe drought in 2018 triggered 371,000 displacements.


On top of conflict and drought, the disruption of international funding, a liquidity crisis and the impacts of the Covid-19 pandemic left Afghanistan at risk of universal poverty by the end year. Disruptions in humanitarian programming, the strain on public services, and climate shocks have exacerbated needs in Afghanistan, where half the population was already in need of humanitarian assistance prior to the Taliban takeover. IDPs live in particularly dire conditions given that their incomes have declined and their assets have been depleted or lost.
