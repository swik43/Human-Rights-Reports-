# Country: Afghanistan

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Afghanistan has suffered conflict and insecurity for more than four decades and experiences recurring disasters and climate shocks. Displacement is a common coping strategy for many Afghans and, in some cases, an inevitable feature of life across generations. Nearly 6.6 million people were living in internal displacement in Afghanistan as of December 2022, two-thirds of them as a result of conflict and violence and a third as a result of disasters. The country has the largest number of IDPs in South Asia, and the second largest worldwide after Syria.


As the Taliban took control of the country on 15 August 2021 and foreign forces withdrew, large-scale conflict and the number of associated displacements reduced significantly, from 723,000 in 2021 to 32,000 in 2022. The reduction in violence has also led to an increase in returns. More than 4.6 million people went home between January 2021 and April 2022, the last data point available, nearly half the figure recorded since 2012. Despite a reduction in conflict-related displacement, the number of conflict IDPs increased in 2022 as compared to the previous year, as it is not possible to ascertain the sustainability of such returns.


Along with the rest of the Afghan population, they still face one of the world’s most acute humanitarian crises, fuelled by steep rises in unemployment, poverty and food insecurity. Women and girls in particular have seen their movements and access to services restricted. A ban on women workers in the aid sector has translated in the suspension of humanitarian activity and further restricted assistance available to Afghan women and children.


In terms of disasters, most displacements reported in 2022 were triggered by earthquakes, totalling 147,000. The province of Badghis was hit by a 5.3 magnitude earthquake on 17 January. It damaged or destroyed almost 1,000 homes and triggered more than 21,000 displacements. Heavy rainfall had weakened mud-brick houses in the preceding days, making them more susceptible to collapse. The earthquake also struck in the middle of winter, exposing those displaced to snow and extreme cold and heightening their needs.


A second earthquake struck the country on 22 June. Considered the deadliest in 20 years, the shallow 5.9 magnitude event and its aftershocks triggered around 118,000 displacements across the eastern provinces of Khost, Paktika and Paktia. Around 1,000 people were killed, and 70 per cent of homes were damaged or destroyed in some areas. Rainfall in the days after the earthquake increased the risk of landslides and flooding and slowed the humanitarian response.


Badghis is one of Afghanistan’s most impoverished provinces, having been affected by drought in recent years and a surge in conflict in 2021. Communities affected by the earthquake in Khost and Paktika already had high humanitarian needs as a result of the country’s economic crisis, and many people expressed concern about not being able to afford to rebuild.
