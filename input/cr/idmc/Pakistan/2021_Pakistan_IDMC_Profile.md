# Country: Pakistan

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Pakistan’s diverse geography and climate make it highly prone to a wide range of hazards. Displacement risk is significant as a result, also driven by the number of people exposed and their socioeconomic vulnerability. Violence has also affected the country for decades, contributing to new and protracted displacement.


Around 70,000 internal displacements were recorded in 2021, all of them triggered by disasters. By far the most significant event was the 5.9 magnitude Harnai earthquake in Balochistan province. Pakistan is in a seismically active zone, but little of its infrastructure is designed to withstand earthquakes. Most homes in Harnai district are made of mud and stone, and thousands were severely damaged or destroyed when the quake struck on 7 October, triggering nearly 68,000 new movements.


Atmospheric conditions brought on by La Niña reduced rainfall and led to moderate drought across the country, decreasing weather-related hazards and triggering fewer displacements than in previous years.  Less snowfall than usual led to a reduction in avalanches and glacial lake outburst floods, which often lead to evacuations and destroy homes during the winter and spring months.


The effects of La Niña also carried over into the July to September monsoon season, when only 2,000 internal displacements were recorded, a significant drop from the five-year average of 167,000. The latter figure, however, is skewed by the 2020 monsoon season which was particularly intense and triggered 810,000 displacements. Sindh province, which tends to record the highest levels of displacement in the country because of its population size and flood risk, recorded a significant rainfall deficit in 2021. Some districts did not record a single drop of rain in August.


Conflict displacement is difficult to record in Pakistan because there is no centralised reporting system and media reporting tends to be politicised. Most has historically tended to occur in the disputed Kashmir region, but a February 2021 agreement between Pakistan and India led to a significant reduction in violence along the line of control and in particular cross-border shelling, which is the primary trigger of displacement in the region.


Pakistan also experiences intercommunal and political violence, and protests and attacks increased in 2021, particularly driven by the Tehreek-e-Labiak Pakistan political party and their supporters. No associated displacement was recorded, but the risk of the violence escalating in future is high.


Beyond new displacement, thousands of people have been living in internal displacement since 2014 as a result of military operations in the former Federally Administered Tribal Area (FATA). The number of IDPs has fallen in recent years but at least 98,000 were still unable to return or achieve other forms of durable solutions as of 2020.
