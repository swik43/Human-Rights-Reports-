# Country: Indonesia

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Indonesia is made up of more than 17,000 islands, all prone to disasters. Rapid population growth and urbanisation have concentrated millions of people in highly exposed areas, and some communities’ social and economic conditions make them more vulnerable to the impacts of natural hazards. These factors combine to fuel some of the highest annual figures for disaster displacement worldwide. The country does, however, invest in disaster risk management, and most movements are in the form of pre-emptive evacuations of people at risk who take refuge in government-run shelters and centres.


Disasters triggered 308,000 internal displacements in 2022, a significant decrease from the 749,000 reported in 2021 as the monsoon season was less pronounced in most of Southeast Asia. Instead, 2022 was marked by disaster displacements due to geophysical hazards. Indeed, more than half of them, around 168,000, were triggered by seven earthquakes. The most significant one was a 5.6 magnitude earthquake which struck near the town of Cianjur in West Java on 21 November, triggering almost 115,000 displacements. Landslides cut off many communities, leaving them without access to essential services. Aftershocks and seasonal rains further hampered rescue and relief efforts.


The second event triggering most displacement was a 5.8 magnitude earthquake that hit Majene Regency in the Mamuju area of West Sulawesi on 8 June, triggering nearly 28,000 displacements. Some residents took precautionary flight to higher grounds fearing aftershocks and potential tsunami waves. The region of West Sumatra experienced three earthquakes throughout the year, totalling almost 24,000 displacements.


The remainder of disaster displacements were mostly due to floods, which triggered almost 135,000 movements. Almost half of annual floods, more than 59,000, took place on 31 December in five northern regencies of Central Java. Limited drainage capacity meant the heavy rains caused widespread flooding, which continued into the first months of 2023. Floods linked to the rainy season, usually between October and March, triggered more than 20,000 displacements, a significant reduction from the 411,000 reported in 2021.


As much fewer new displacements were recorded in 2022, the number of people living in displacement as a result of disasters in the country reduced by more than half in a year, reaching 68,000.


Violence also triggered four times fewer than in 2021, with 7,100 movements. The country’s parliament approved legislation in June to form three new provinces – South Papua, Central Papua and Highlands Papua in the easternmost region of Papua. The move was met with protests, triggering violence and displacement in the following months. Around 72,000 people were living in displacement as a result of conflict and violence nationwide at the end of 2022, almost all of which were living in a protracted situation.
