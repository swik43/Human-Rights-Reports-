# Country: Iraq

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Iraq has a long history of internal displacement rooted in intercommunal tensions and fed by almost two decades of conflict, including the recent conflict with the Islamic State in Iraq and the Levant (ISIL). The country is also prone to hazards including floods, drought and earthquakes that force people from their homes.


In 2022, the country continued to suffer its worst drought in 40 years, and increases in water and food prices had a disproportionate effect on those displaced. Drought triggered nearly 51,000 displacements across eight governorates, in particular Thi-Qar where the Mesopotamian Marshes are located. The wetlands are home to large communities of water buffalo herders whose livelihoods were severely affected by livestock deaths.


The drought also led to a drop in food production and a rise in prices that undermined the livelihoods of farmers and herders more broadly, particularly in the south. Poor water management and the building of upstream dams served to aggravate the situation. Nearly 69,000 people were living in internal displacement as a result of drought at the end of 2022. They were spread across more than half of the country’s governorates, with Missan hosting the largest number.


Pockets of violence triggered over 32,000 displacements in 2022, almost half the 2021 figure. A third took place in northern Iraq in early May when the security forces clashed with non-state armed groups. Few have returned to their homes, in part given a lack of livelihood opportunities, delays in compensation for damaged housing and the trauma of repeated displacement and violence.


Countrywide, the number of returns since the defeat of ISIL in 2017 have been significant, but challenges remain, including lack of tenure security and the prevalence of non-state armed groups. Latest figures showed that more than 592,000 returnees were living in conditions of poverty and vulnerability, exposed to protection risks including exploitation and abuse, gender-based violence and human trafficking. Greater efforts to improve security and provide missing civil documents, adequate housing, public services and livelihood opportunities are needed to make returns sustainable.


Around 1.2 million people were still living in displacement across the country as of the end of 2022, the majority in informal settings outside camps. Unemployment rates among IDPs in Iraq are at least ten per cent higher than among their non-displaced counterparts, which in turn heightens their food insecurity. This has pushed many into adopting negative coping strategies including taking their children out of school, selling productive assets, becoming involved in crime, and early marriage.
