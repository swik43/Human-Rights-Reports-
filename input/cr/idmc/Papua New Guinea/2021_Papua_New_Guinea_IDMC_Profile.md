# Country: Papua New Guinea

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Papua New Guinea is among the countries most at risk of disasters worldwide because of its exposure to floods, earthquakes and volcanic eruptions. Social and economic vulnerability are also factors in driving thousands of displacements every year. The country has a long history of intercommunal violence fuelled by issues ranging from customary land ownership to ethnic and cultural rivalry that triggers displacement as well.


Violence triggered 9,500 internal displacements in 2021, the highest figure ever recorded for the country. Intercommunal violence in Eastern Highland province in April accounted for more than 5,000. Criminal violence driven by poverty, unemployment and the effects of the Covid-19 pandemic also led to a surge in displacement in urban areas.


Frustration over income inequality and lack of work opportunities has long been a driver of urban crime and violence, particularly among young men, and the situation deteriorated last year in a number of cities. Around 1,000 displacements were recorded as a result of criminal violence, a figure that should be read as conservative given that the phenomenon goes largely unreported.


Disasters triggered more than 7,500 displacements in 2021, almost twice as many as in 2020. The most significant event was in December when unusually high tides washed away thousands of coastal homes. Around 7,200 displacements were recorded in East Sepik province alone. La Niña and sea level rise brought on by climate change contributed to the severity of this naturally occurring event.


Around 41,000 people were living in displacement as a result of violence and disasters at the end of year, of whom 70 per cent were displaced between five and 17 years ago and have been unable to achieve durable solutions since. Many live in areas prone to disasters, where they are exposed to the risk of further displacement. Tensions with host communities over resources also often lead to repeated displacement and heightened needs.


Security vacuums, poverty and the absence of legislation on internal displacement are among the many obstacles to breaking Papua New Guinea's cycles of violence and addressing disaster displacement in urban and hard-to-reach areas. Initiatives such as the Highlands Joint Programme 2020-2022 and the Urban Youth Employment Project hold the promise of addressing some of the drivers of violence. An ongoing assessment of prospects for durable solutions could also play a significant role in shaping the country’s first policy on internal displacement.
