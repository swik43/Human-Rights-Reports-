# Country: Myanmar (Burma)

---

## Year: 2023

### Country Profile

In February 2021, a military takeover of the government sparked protests that were violently supressed. The demonstrations, however, quickly evolved into an armed resistance movement that spread across the country. Since, conflict displacement has risen almost three-fold, adding a layer of complexity to long-running conflicts between the military and a number of ethnic non-state armed groups.


The number of displacements associated with conflict and violence increased throughout 2023, but particularly after 27 October when a coalition of NSAGs in northern areas of Shan state decided to conduct coordinated attacks against the military. A rapid escalation in fighting and displacement ensued and spread to other areas, where NSAGs took control of several towns and military outposts and the military conducted retaliatory attacks.


Around half of the displacements recorded during 2023, or 670,000, took place in November and December, particularly in Sagaing, Shan and Rakhine states. Sagaing was already hosting the largest number of IDPs fleeing conflict in the country, and the new wave of violence increased their number to 1.1 million as of the end of 2023, 41 per cent of the national total.


In the western state of Rakhine, the Arakan Army, the main NSAG in the state, took advantage of the security situation in other regions to conduct attacks against the military, leading to a rise in displacement from mid-November 2023. Rakhine was among the few states where a reduction in the number of IDPs was observed in 2022, in part because of an informal ceasefire between the military and the Arakan Army. The new wave of violence in November triggered 124,000 displacements, leaving 325,000 people living in displacement in the state as of the end of 2023.


The escalating conflict meant that many people were forced to flee repeatedly. Access restrictions posed significant challenges to humanitarian organisations and camp closures left many IDPs in precarious conditions, particularly in the north-east and south-east of the country and in Rakhine state.


Additionally, the country was hit by one of the most significant disasters in the world in 2023: cyclone Mocha.It made landfall in Myanmar on 14 May as a category 4-equivalent storm, triggering 912,000 internal displacements. It struck Sittwe, the capital of Rakhine state, and damaged homes across the state. At least 63,000 displacements took place from camps sheltering people already displaced by conflict, including Rohingya communities living in protracted displacement.


The cyclone also caused significant agricultural damage and led to increased food insecurity among internally displaced people (IDPs). This, coupled with access constraints associated with conflict, heightened overall humanitarian needs, particularly in areas hosting large numbers of IDPs, such as Rakhine and Chin states.


Around 2.9 million people were living in internal displacement in Myanmar at the end of 2023, up from the 1.5 million at the end of 2022. Over 2.6 million were displaced by conflict and violence and 235,000 by disasters.
