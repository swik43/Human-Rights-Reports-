# Country: Mali

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Mali is in the throes one of the largest internal displacement crises in Africa, along with neighbouring Burkina Faso and Niger, and the triggers and drivers of displacement in the Sahelian country are complex and often intertwined. The crisis has its roots in long-standing grievances between communities and against the state that escalated in 2012 and 2013, particularly in the northern regions. Conflict and violence has expanded from Timbuktu toward the central regions of Mopti and Gao since 2018.


This expansion triggered more than 249,000 internal displacements in 2021, one of the highest figures recorded for the country in a decade. Security conditions deteriorated in a climate of political instability accentuated by a military takeover in May, and armed attacks by non-state armed groups (NSAGs) against civilians accounted for two-thirds of the total. France also announced in mid-2021 that it would reduce its military presence in the country, which it did in early 2022 when its forces withdrew. The presence of Russian military personnel started to increase at the same time.


Clashes between NSAGs also triggered significant displacement in 2021, as did intercommunal violence over land and natural resources in Timbuktu, Mopti and Ménaka. A series of local peace agreements brought greater stability and a temporary lull in violence to some regions, but communities continued to clash. This was the case in the Djenné and Niono cercles of Mopti, where a local ceasefire failed after several months, and conflict flared again in October.


Floods triggered around 6,000 internal displacements in 2021. Mali also faces prolonged periods of drought that affect food security and livelihoods, but data on displacement associated with this and other slow-onset phenomena is currently unavailable. What is known, however, is that the number of food insecure people rose to more than 1.3 million in 2021, a three-fold increase on 2020 and the highest figure since 2012.


The biggest increases in internal displacement were recorded in the regions of Gao, Kidal, Mopti, Koulikoro and Ségou, which host three-quarters of the country’s IDPs. This shows how the impacts of disasters combine with those of conflict and violence to aggravate displaced people’s living conditions.


Around 350,000 people were living in internal displacement in Mali as of the end of 2021, 326,000 as a result of conflict and violence and 24,000 as a result of disasters. Most IDPs live in crowded homes with host families and communities or in temporary informal sites.
