# Country: Nepal

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Monsoon rains cause floods and landslides that displace people across Nepal every year. The country also sits at the convergence of the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates, making it highly prone to earthquakes. The 2015 Gorkha event was the most significant in its recent history and led to around 2.6 million internal displacements.


Disasters triggered 32,000 displacements in 2021. Most occurred during the June to September monsoon season, but other hazards also forced people from their homes during the year. An unprecedented wildfire season spread from forests into human settlements in March and April, destroying 55 homes in Koshi and Lumbini provinces and leading to 250 movements.


The fires also intensified the impact of the monsoon by destroying vegetation in many flood-prone areas. The reduction in the ground’s ability to absorb rainfall increased the risk of floods and landslides. Around 18,000 internal displacements had been recorded by the end of the season, including around 2,200 when a landslide blocked the Melamchi river, causing a dam to burst in Bagmati province on 15 June.


Disasters triggered fewer internal displacements in 2021 than in previous years, primarily the result of a less intense monsoon season. Unlike previous years, however, a similar number of displacements occurred outside it. Severe flooding and landslides led to nearly 15,000 in Koshi, Karnali and Sudurpashchim provinces between 17 and 21 October. The unseasonal rainfall came at the beginning of the harvest season and devastated the livelihoods of thousands of families already hard-hit by the monsoon and the Covid-19 pandemic.


It is estimated that at least 8,400 people remain displaced as of 31 December 2021 due to monsoon impacts. While most disasters in Nepal are small in scale and the upheaval, they trigger is temporary, the Ghorka earthquake led to protracted displacement. A reconstruction programme for affected families concluded in November 2021 after the construction of 50,000 homes in Ghorka and Nuwakot provinces.  This was a significant step in securing durable solutions, but more homes are still in need of rebuilding. 


The 2015 earthquake also had a lasting impact on Nepal’s geography, contributing to more frequent landslides and driving landslide risk in new areas. Changing climate patterns are affecting the wider Himalayan region too, increasing landslide and flood risk which could in turn fuel displacement in the future.


Armed conflict in Nepal officially ended in 2006, and all IDPs who fled the fighting were confirmed as having achieved durable solutions.
