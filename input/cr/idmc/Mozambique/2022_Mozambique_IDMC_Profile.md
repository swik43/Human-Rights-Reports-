# Country: Mozambique

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Conflict and violence in Mozambique’s northern province of Cabo Delgado escalated in 2017 and triggered hundreds of thousands of displacements in the years that followed, both within the province and to neighbouring ones. The increase in violence and displacement prompted the government to call for regional military support. Military gains enabled the initiation of peacebuilding activities.


While security incidents reduced in the first quarter of 2022, violence reignited to become even more frequent than in 2021 in Cabo Delgado, the neighbouring province of Nampula and, to a lesser extent, Niassa. Nearly 283,000 movements were recorded, over 50 per cent more than in 2021. As Nampula already hosted a large number of IDPs fleeing violence from Cabo Delgado, the attacks forced displaced people to flee again. Half of IDPs assessed in 2022 reported having been displaced multiple times and 14 per cent reported having fled four times or more.


Infrastructure has also been damaged by fighting in areas controlled by non-state armed groups in Cabo Delgado, which has particularly affected access to water and hygiene infrastructure. This has worsened access to health care, which is already limited by the reduced number of medical staff.


As 2022 concluded, over a million people were living in internal displacement as a result of conflict and violence across the country, a 40 per cent increase from 2021. The protracted nature of the displacement and the rapid increase of the internally displaced population is putting additional strain on host communities’ scarce resources and increasing their needs.


In terms of disasters, Mozambique continues to be affected by droughts, floods and storms. In January 2022, tropical storm Ana triggered close to 89,000 displacements, while 24,000 more displacements were recorded in early March after tropical cyclone Gombe struck Nampula province, which hosts people already internally displaced by conflict and violence. The consecutive storms have negatively impacted agricultural production and increased food insecurity.


Mirroring trends from previous years, many disasters affected communities already displaced. Around 127,000 people were living in displacement as a result of disasters at the end of 2022. Two-thirds of them, almost 85,000, have been living in displacement since Cyclone Idai hit the country in 2019. Another 37,000 have been displaced since 2021 following tropical storm Eloise.
