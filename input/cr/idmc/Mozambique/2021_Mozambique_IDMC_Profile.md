# Country: Mozambique

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Conflict and violence in Mozambique’s northern province of Cabo Delgado escalated dramatically between 2017 and 2020, displacing hundreds of thousands of people both within the province and to neighbouring ones. There were 187,000 new movements in 2021, nearly three times fewer than in 2020 as security improved in some areas. Around 735,000 people were living in displacement at the end of the year, up from 676,000 at the end of 2020 and 10,000 at the end of 2017.


The main displacement event in 2021 was an attack by non-state armed groups (NSAGs) on Palma district in late March that triggered 92,000 new movements. It also prompted a regional military intervention by several member states of the Southern African Development Community (SADC) and a bilateral deployment from Rwanda. With their support, the national army retook many areas of Cabo Delgado controlled by NSAGs after four years of conflict.


Smaller-scale attacks continued to take place, but the government also approved a three-year reconstruction plan for the province and announced it would start returning IDPs to towns it had retaken. Nearly 50,000 went back to their homes between July 2021 and January 2022, two-thirds of them citing improved security as their main reason for doing so.


Disasters, mainly storms and floods, triggered around 44,000 displacements in 2021. Tropical storm Chalane struck the central provinces of Sofala and Manica in late December 2020 and the first days of January 2021. It caused severe damage to homes and shelters, many in sites hosting people displaced by previous events such as cyclones Idai and Kenneth in 2019. Chalane was followed a few weeks later by cyclone Eloise, which hit central and southern regions in late January. Across the country as a whole 138,000 people were living in displacement as a result of disasters at the end of the year.


Fifty-two per cent of Mozambique’s IDPs are children and 27 per cent women. Around four out of five live in host communities. Displacement impacts are generally severe and protection risks are a concern, particularly for women, girls, elderly people, those with disabilities and those living with HIV/AIDS. Conflict and violence have deepened food insecurity as farmers were forced to abandon their fields, and erratic rainfall has added to crop losses in some parts of the country. Around 1.9 million people faced acute food insecurity as of November 2021, of whom 1.3 million were in the northern provinces of Cabo Delgado, Niassa, Nampula and Zambezia, which host most of the country’s IDPs.
