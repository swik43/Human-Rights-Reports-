# Country: Sri Lanka

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Disasters are the primary trigger of displacement in Sri Lanka. Peaks tend to occur at the onset of the south-west monsoon in May or June and the north-east monsoon in September or October. 


More than 121,000 internal displacements were recorded in 2021, a significant increase on the figure for 2020 and the highest recorded since 2017. Two widespread flooding events accounted for most of the displacements. Heavy monsoon rains led to floods that triggered 50,000 during the first week of June. Many of the districts affected, such as Colombo, Gampaha, Kegalle and Kalutara, had also been flooded weeks earlier. Floods and landslides struck eight of the country’s nine provinces again in November, triggering more than 65,000 movements, many of them in the same areas as had been affected in June.


Localised gales, floods and landslides also lead to displacement outside the monsoon season. Such small-scale disasters triggered around 800 displacements in 2021, most often fewer than 60 at a time.


Disaster displacement tends to be short term, and most people return to their homes once floodwaters recede or the risk of landslides subsides. Sri Lanka is highly exposed to hazards, however, and they are distributed unevenly, meaning that many people from the same areas are forced to leave their homes repeatedly.


Religious tensions fuel small-scale incidents of intercommunal violence, but no related displacements have been recorded since 2019. The country’s civil war ended in 2019, but around 11,000 people were still living in displacement at the end of 2021 as a result of it.
