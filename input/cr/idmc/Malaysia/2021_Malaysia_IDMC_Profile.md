# Country: Malaysia

---

## Year: 2021

### Country Profile

Malaysia has relatively low levels of disaster risk compared with other countries in the East Asia and Pacific region. It is not in the typhoon belt, and its location near the Pacific ring of fire does not pose a significant seismic risk. That said, floods, landslides, drought and wildfires do take place, with the former triggering most internal displacements.


Unsustainable human practices including deforestation, land degradation and the development of densely populated areas on flood plains without adequate drainage systems have played a role in increasing flood damages, losses and displacement risk in recent years. Most tend to take place during the annual monsoon season from October to March.


Floods triggered around 129,000 internal displacements in 2021, compared with 24,000 in 2020. The most significant occurred at the end of the year, when around 68,000 new movements were recorded across ten states. Much of the rainfall was associated with tropical depression 29W, which dumped 316.5 mm of rainfall on Klang district in Selangor state on 18 December. This compares with average monthly rainfall of 202 mm for the country as a whole. The three worst affected states were Pahang, which recorded 34,000 displacements, Selangor 32,000 and Kelantan 1,600.


Another major event occurred at the start of the year, when torrential rains caused rivers to burst their banks, flooding hundreds of communities across seven eastern and southern states and triggering 43,000 displacements between 3 and 19 January.


Movement restrictions imposed to curb the spread of Covid-19 hindered evacuation and rescue efforts in both cases.


Around 7,000 people were still living in displacement in Malaysia as a result of floods as of the end of the year.
