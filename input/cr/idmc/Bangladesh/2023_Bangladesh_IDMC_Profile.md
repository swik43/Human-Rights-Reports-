# Country: Bangladesh

---

## Year: 2023

### Country Profile

Bangladesh’s location and high population density make it one of the world’s most disaster-prone countries. Disasters are the main trigger of displacement in the country, particularly during the June-September monsoon season.


Cyclone Mocha was the largest weather-related disaster displacement event in the world in 2023. The storm formed in the Bay of Bengal on 11 May and triggered 1.3 million displacements in Bangladesh, mostly in Chattogram division’s Cox’s Bazar district. Almost all the movements were pre-emptive evacuations from densely populated areas. Forecasting and early warnings helped authorities to put emergency procedures in place a week before Mocha’s landfall.


For example, St Martin’s Island was evacuated to save lives, and boats were pulled ashore to protect tourism and fishing livelihoods. However, the cyclone destroyed around 1,200 houses, leaving about 4,900 people, almost half of the island’s population, facing prolonged displacement.


Mocha also affected vulnerable communities including Rohingya refugees from Myanmar living in Cox’s Bazar. About 30,000 refugees were temporarily relocated from their homes. Many had been living in informal shelters made of materials unable to withstand Mocha’s impact, impeding their swift return and recovery. The storm destroyed around 3,300 homes in and around Cox’s Bazar, leaving more than 13,000 people living in internal displacement at the end of 2023.


Cyclone Hamoon struck the same locations later in the year. It made landfall on 24 October and led to 273,000 evacuations. Rohingya refugees were again among those displaced.


Bangladesh also recorded a significant decrease in flood displacements to 213,000, about half the decadal average. This decline is even more striking given that all the movements took place on the same day and in the same place: 5 August in Cox’s Bazar.


Major storms and floods have hit exposed communities in the Chattogram division year after year, forcing them to move repeatedly. The Bangladeshi government and its partners have made significant progress in reducing the impacts of disasters, but their recurrent nature underscores the need to continue strengthening disaster risk reduction and early warning measures.


Political and intercommunal violence regularly trigger localised displacements in Bangladesh, but the phenomenon is likely to be under-reported because there are no official sources collecting data on it. No new displacements were recorded in 2023 and no updates were provided on the situation of people living in protracted displacement due to conflict and violence.


The majority of the 426,000 people still displaced by conflict and violence as of the end of 2023 in Bangladesh have been displaced for decades. The majority fled conflict that took place in the Chittagong Hill Tracts from the 1970s until 1997 between government forces and Shanti Bahini, the armed wing of the indigenous peoples’ political party, Parbatya Chattagram Jana Samhati Samiti.
