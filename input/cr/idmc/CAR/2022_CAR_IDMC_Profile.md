# Country: Central African Republic

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Internal displacement has been prevalent in the Central African Republic (CAR) for decades, mostly due to conflict and violence.  In 2013, a coalition of non-state armed groups (NSAGs) known as Séléka took over the country’s capital, Bangui. The conflict triggered a high number of displacements during the following years. In February 2019, the government and 14 NSAGs signed the Political Agreement for Peace and Reconciliation in the Central African Republic, the eighth deal of its kind since 2012.


  However, several of the signatories to the 2019 peace deal united under the Coalition of Patriots for Change (CPC) and reignited conflict. As a result, a significant increase in displacement was observed in 2020 and 2021. The country’s president declared a unilateral ceasefire with NSAGs in October 2021, raising hopes of renewed efforts to implement the 2019 peace deal. In March 2022, he convened a political dialogue in an attempt to improve the situation, but some of the main opposition groups did not participate. 


Still, the country’s armed forces were able to solidify control of major urban centres during 2022, which allowed some IDPs to return home. As clashes between the armed forces and NSAGs moved away from populated urban areas, the number of displacements dropped by 40 per cent compared to 2021, totalling 290,000. This figure includes displacements associated with intercommunal violence, approximately 5,000 displacements in the Basse-Kotto prefecture, as well as 1,800 in Haute-Kotto and 1,300 in Ouaka prefectures.  


As NSAGs dispersed in rural areas, they continued to perpetrate acts of violence that triggered displacement. While their activities were less coordinated and more localised, extorsion and harassment of local populations, gender-based violence and recruitment of children were still reported.


The humanitarian situation in the CAR remains challenging, with almost half of the population acutely food insecure. Additionally, 75 per cent of IDPs live in host families rather than in camp sites, sometimes putting a strain on host communities who themselves lack resources. Both IDPs and their hosts reportedly had to reduce their use of health services as a coping strategy to afford food in 2022.


As of the end of 2022, 516,000 people remained internally displaced by conflict and violence. The vast majority of them had been displaced for two years or even longer. This is however the lowest figure since 2016 as security improvements increased the number of returns. Improved humanitarian access also permitted greater coverage in data collection and allowed to paint a clearer picture of returns during 2018 and 2019. This helped bring the end of year figure for 2022 to a more accurate estimate. The sustainability of these returns is however not well-documented and some reports have highlighted the lack of services and infrastructure in areas of origin.


In terms of disasters, the country was hit by numerous floods and strong winds which triggered 77,000 displacements throughout 2022, the second highest figure ever recorded for the country. The most significant displacements were reported following floods in the capital of Bangui in July and September, when 23,000 and 22,000 displacements were recorded, respectively. It remained uncertain how many people remained displaced as 2022 concluded.
