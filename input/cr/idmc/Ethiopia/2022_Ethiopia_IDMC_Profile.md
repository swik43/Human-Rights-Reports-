# Country: Ethiopia

---

## Year: 2022

### Country Profile

Conflict and violence triggered more than two million internal displacements in 2022, a 60 per cent decrease from the 5.1 million recorded in 2021 when escalating conflict in northern regions, and particularly Tigray, pushed the figure to an all-time high.


The government announced a humanitarian truce in March 2022 and, despite a resumption of fighting in August, the parties signed a peace agreement in November, raising hopes for those displaced to find durable solutions. The agreement also enabled the establishment of four humanitarian aid corridors into Tigray from neighbouring Afar and Amhara regions. Thousands of trucks delivered aid, mostly food, preventing the food security situation from deteriorating further.


While the number of internal displacements decreased, the number people living in displacement as a result of conflict and violence at the end of the year slightly increased in 2022, reaching nearly 3.9 million, the third highest figure in sub-Saharan Africa. Growing conflict and violence in Oromia has contributed to this increase. This high figure illustrates the persistent challenges to return even after the signing of a peace agreement and the need to invest in durable solutions.


In terms of disasters, the number of displacements increased significantly as the country recorded 873,000 movements, more than a three-fold increase from 2021. Floods in August and October triggered 185,000 displacements in the western region of Gambella. The bulk of the disaster figure, however, is attributed to the drought which affected the Horn of Africa. Ethiopia endured a fifth consecutive failed rainy season, in part due to a prolonged La Niña phenomenon. Consequently, approximately 686,000 drought displacements were recorded. The situation was particularly acute in the regions of Somali and Oromia, which were also affected by conflict and violence.


The compounding impacts of drought and conflict are particularly noticeable in terms of food insecurity in northern Ethiopia, were 80 per cent of the population relied on agriculture as their main source of income and food before conflict erupted in November 2020. As the conflict unfolded, crops were burned and pillaged, forcing farmers to abandon their land. Agricultural tools and livestock were destroyed, access to fields was restricted and the transport of food was blocked.


Some displaced farmers were able to return after the truce in March 2022, but damage to their land and equipment continued to hinder food production. Most of the planting season had already passed by the time they returned, and this, combined with the extended drought, prevented farmers from beginning a new production cycle.


Reliance on food assistance was high in all areas affected by the conflict in 2022 as a result, but numerous challenges, including blockades and looting, impeded the provision of aid.
